The Litzkow Family of Madison, Wisconsin

This site exists to preserve and share a collection of letters, documents, photographs, and ephemera belonging to the Litzkow family of 1213 Hickory Street, Madison, Wisconsin — artifacts spanning the World War II era and its aftermath. They are presented here for family members, researchers, and anyone who wishes to glimpse the lives of ordinary people in extraordinary times.

What you will find here is not a complete account of these men's military service, nor a comprehensive family history. It is simply a glimpse into the items they chose to keep — the letters they saved, the documents they folded into drawers, the postcards they tucked away. Much remains unknown, and more history may be added as it is discovered.

Otto John Litzkow (February 11, 1902 – January 10, 1966) served with the United States Navy Seabees, CBMU-587, in the Pacific Theater. James Robert Litzkow (June 6, 1926 – May 29, 1980) served with the US Army Air Forces during the war, training at bases in Texas before going overseas. Lorraine Josephine Litzkow (January 22, 1906 – July 23, 1983) held the family together at home in Madison. These are their letters.

After 12 months with the Seabees on the Solomon Islands, Otto J. Litzkow, chief ship fitter, has been transferred to the Admiralty Islands. Husband of Lorraine J. Litzkow, 1213 Hickory St., Chief Litzkow enlisted Oct. 30, 1943 and trained at Camp Rousseau and Port Hueneme, Calif.

✦
Historical Context

This newspaper clipping — likely from the Wisconsin State Journal or Capital Times — is one of the most significant documents in the collection. It contains the only known photographs of both Otto and James, and confirms the remarkable fact that father and son were serving simultaneously: Otto in the Pacific with the Seabees, James in Texas with the Army Air Forces.

The article reveals that Otto enlisted October 30, 1943 and trained at Camp Rousseau, New Jersey and Port Hueneme, California before shipping out. His Pacific posting took him first to the Solomon Islands for twelve months before transfer to the Admiralty Islands.

James is described as training as an aerial mechanic — consistent with his Amarillo postings — and is noted as a former member of the Madison Civil Air Patrol and the Wisconsin State National Guard. His attendance at Madison West High School explains the Capital Times sports clipping found elsewhere in this collection.

Bryan Army Air Field  ·  August 15, 1944

Hi Slugger,

This is really what you call delayed action. I'm sorry as hell, but can offer no excuses other than I haven't had a helluva lot a' free time nor a lot of ambition. I guess I just like to receive better than to send.

I am not sure, but I don't believe you'll be gone — nor will you be going for a long time if things turn out as they look to be. There is even a wild rumor that Cadets are going to be returned to inactive status in 30–60 days. It is true that some cadets were sent home last month, but I don't know what the circumstances were exactly. It's just a rumor so far, but a lot of speculation has been made about it. Walter Winchell mentioned something to the effect that within 60 days cadets in the air corps were going to receive the surprise of their lives. Just exactly what he meant, I don't know, but it must come off before so very long as he said that 3 or so weeks ago.

Damnit anyhow, I'm broke again. I lost my last $25 in a crap game a couple days ago & now am on borrowed dough. I was going to go to Benton last week-end, but I resprained my ankle & had to call it off. I s'pose you know I either lost or had my Parker-51 pen & a pr. of $14 sun glasses swiped? I've been running into a streak of bad luck that'd frost the balls right off your hat Captain.

This field is really becoming a crock of shit tho. — Chicken shit they call it. We're treated like low-class monkeys & called too valuable to go up in planes. They kick our asses all over & then make us stay in this damn camp most of the time. I'm doin' a good job a griping, huh? Well, everyone is really pissed off down here. Guess that's why.

I'm on the section baseball team & we have a game tomorrow nite. We have to go into Bryan to play. I'm gonna get ahold of some nice little dish & ketch up on lost time one of these days. I mean really ketch up too. Tell that goddam Klinefelter he can go take the running jump if he won't write me with him. I don't know what the hell the trouble is, but I sure don't like being kept in the dark. Make that bastard write me!!

[ continued · pages 2–3 ]

Just like before, Small-fry.

Historical Context

Otto signed this letter "Small-fry" — the father writing to his son Jim, whom he addressed as "Slugger." At the time, James was 18 years old and stationed at Bryan Army Air Field in Texas, a major WWII pilot training base. Both men were in uniform simultaneously, leaving Lorraine alone in Madison.

Jim's reference to Walter Winchell — the era's most powerful radio broadcaster — hinting at cadet reassignments reflects real uncertainty gripping the Air Corps in mid-1944 as the war's demands shifted. The Parker 51 fountain pen he mentions losing was considered the finest pen money could buy at the time, worth roughly $245 in today's dollars — a significant loss for a young serviceman on a private's pay.

Jim demands his father tell Klinefelter to write him — the same Klinefelter who later sent a packet of Fort Jackson, South Carolina postcards to Lorraine in December 1945, confirming he was a real and ongoing presence in the family's wartime circle.

Hi Mom,

I'll bet that you will be surprised to see this typed letter for once. Well I got fucked into this detail of runner for the orderly for tonight. We just finished another branch today and have to pull a detail after every branch so I got this. The only bad part of it is that I have been up since 6 this morning and am on until 8 A.M. tomorrow or should I say today. As it is now about 1 A.M.

I received a box of candy from you and Hellen today. Thanks a lot. The candy sure comes in handy during the breaks at school. In about 7 hours time one gets pretty hungry and that is for sure.

I wrote one letter tonight to Lillian already. I spread her the same old shit as usual. There isn't much to write about here so that is just about all that I can do is to spread a little of that stuff around. I'll have to get one off to Helen yet and thank her for the candy. That is if I don't get to tired to do any more. I haven't been getting very much mail lately. I don't know if they have been screwing it up in the mail room or just what. John got a letter yesterday that has been following him around for a year. Fast service no?

Love, Jim

Historical Context

By March 1945, James had been transferred from Bryan to Amarillo Army Air Field — home of the 3701st AAF Base Unit. This typed letter, written on official base stationery at 1 A.M. while pulling orderly duty, is a small window into the tedium of military life between training sessions.

James mentions writing simultaneously to Lillian and Helen — likely girlfriends or pen pals — a common practice among young servicemen keeping morale up across multiple correspondences. His candid, unguarded tone with his mother is notably different from the letter he wrote to his father the previous August.

Just five months after this letter, Lorraine underwent a hysterectomy on August 24, 1945 — prompting the American Red Cross to arrange an emergency furlough to bring James home to Madison. The war in the Pacific ended just weeks later, on September 2, 1945, reuniting the family at last.

Transcription
Admiralty Islands, Pacific  ·  June 10, 1945

Hi Sweetheart,

I am really a broken down old seabee today. I am just about pooped out. It is so hot here today the sweat is just pouring off of me. I can feel it run down my hide in little rivers, and it is not because I have too many clothes on because I am just as naked as the girls in this picture. I just got up to look at the thermometer and I see there is a puddle of water where I was sitting.

So when it gets hot at home there for the next couple of months just let the water run off of you and think nothing of it. I wish I was home for these couple of months, you would keep cool watching an old freeze and shiver. When the thermometer gets up to eighty five and ninety five in the shade just go out in the sun for awhile and start working. Then you can make up your mind whether you would like to live in the tropics or not. I know you never could stand the heat at home without sweating your head off. Then when the water is pouring off of you just try to figure what you would look like after eighteen months with the temperature above eighty, ninety percent of the time day and night.

I thought sure I would get some mail today, we didn't get any yesterday and so far none today, so I suppose we will get a flock of mail again a short time left to wait. And now that the time is going so fast and we are busy. It seems the week is gone before I know it.

I don't doubt that many of the girls figure they have a hard time working for a living, but I really think their work would be a lot easier for them if they would know enough to pick out some type of work they liked to do, and really take an interest in their work. If they would do that it wouldn't be long before they got a promotion and they would enjoy working. I know that when we were on Treasury and our work slacked up I had too much time for thinking and it gets you feeling sorry for yourself so I am glad that we have a lot of work ahead of here.

I don't know about Andrewjeski getting out of the armed forces because he is a welder. You know they can use a lot of welders both in the army and the navy, and they want the good ones. They would have to learn to manage the same as everybody else has had to do.

Lots & lots of love & kisses, not just the usual amount. Otto

Historical Context

Written on "Thinkin' of You" Seabees stationery featuring a cartoon sailor surrounded by pinup pictures, this letter captures the brutal reality of service in the Pacific tropics. Otto's vivid description of the heat — sweating so much he leaves a puddle where he sits — gives a visceral sense of conditions his family at home in Madison could barely imagine.

His mention of the Treasury Islands as a prior posting confirms the deployment sequence documented in the "Father, Son Serve" newspaper clipping: Treasury Islands first, then the Admiralty Islands. The reference to "Andrewjeski" reflects the community of men Otto lived alongside for months on end, whose lives he followed closely.

This is one of three consecutive-day letters Otto sent to Lorraine in June 1945, each on different themed Seabees stationery. His sign-off — "Lots & lots of love & kisses, not just the usual amount" — is characteristic of the warmth that runs throughout this collection.

Historical Context

Otto addresses Lorraine as "my sweet heart Tiny" — a private nickname he used for her consistently throughout his Pacific correspondence. The name appears in multiple letters and was entirely unknown to later generations of the family until these documents were discovered. It may have been Otto's own term of endearment, used only in writing and never spoken aloud at home.

Both the June 10 and June 16 letters were written on the same "Thinkin' of You" Seabees stationery — a cartoon by A.J. Andersen of the 4th NCB showing a sailor reading a letter surrounded by pinup pictures. Otto sent three different Seabees stationery designs to Lorraine over consecutive days in June 1945, each one a small gesture of sustained attention from thousands of miles away.

The Admiralty Islands, where Otto was stationed, are a remote Pacific archipelago north of New Guinea. By June 1945 they had been under Allied control for over a year, serving as a major staging base. Otto's unit, CBMU-587, was a Construction Battalion Maintenance Unit — the Seabees whose motto was "We Build, We Fight." Otto's relaxed humor about Dan Kramer and the neighbors speaks to the remarkable trust he and Lorraine had built. This letter is missing its second page.

Transcription
Fort Bragg, North Carolina  ·  April 30, 1949

Hello Jim,

Well don't fall completely over when you get this letter but I am in the army since Jan. 15. And 2 more weeks and I get my paratrooper wings. The hardest part is over. I'll be stationed at Ft. Bragg, N.C. with the 82nd. I'm not too crazy about this army as yet but I got a long ways to go. Sure wish I was back working for Nass. We had some pretty good times rambling that old truck. What are you doing for excitement these days. Today is payday and everybody is drunk but me, and I'm C.Q. or I would be too. I'm squad leader and put our plt. Sgt. in a P.F.C. so you can see all the rank around here. Ha! Ha!

How is the plumbing coming along. Boy any plums yet. I joined the army because it was the best thing I could do I thought. I got down on the other side of Dallas and got to drinking and you know how I follow the curbs when I'm drunk, well there wasn't any curb so I went looking for one and found a semi instead and so I got out and hitchhiked back to Dallas and then St. Louis and the Finance Co. and Insurance Co. and Texas was looking for me so I thought I better do something so I enlisted.

Say I wonder if you would do me a favor and send my Motor Cycle to me COD when I get to Bragg I can sell it for $425. without batting an eye. There is some rent due on the Garage but I'll take care of that with you but I won't do nothing until I hear from you.

This airborne training isn't the easiest I ever done either. I never run so much before in my life. That's all those people from down here is double-time and it really sweats the old whiskey out of you.

Meet any new girls since I have been gone and who are you running around with these days. As for my share of these Georgia peaches you can have them all. You all.

Well awaiting to hear from you Jim so please answer soon and don't tell everybody you see I'm in the army either O.K.

P.S. I'll put my name on the envelope so you will know what it is.

Your Pal and buddy, Honker

Historical Context

Honker — whose first name does not appear in the letter, only his underlined surname — was clearly a close friend and former workmate of James. They had worked together for someone named "Nass," sharing a truck and what sounds like a lively working life. By 1949 the war was four years past and both men were navigating civilian life with mixed success. Honker's account of his path to enlistment — a drinking session in Dallas, a near-encounter with a semi-truck, and mounting debts with the Finance Company and Insurance Company — is one of the most entertaining passages in the entire collection.

His assignment to the 82nd Airborne at Fort Bragg, then America's premier paratrooper division, suggests genuine ability beneath the colorful lifestyle. The request to ship his motorcycle COD so he can sell it for $425 — roughly $5,600 in today's dollars — is a small masterpiece of practical optimism.

This letter is the latest dated item in the collection, and the only one not directly connected to the WWII years — a window into the post-war lives of the men who had served.

Otto & the Seabees

Otto John Litzkow — Chief Ship Fitter, CBMU-587

Otto John Litzkow enlisted on October 30, 1943, and was assigned to Construction Battalion Maintenance Unit 587 of the United States Naval Reserve — the Seabees, whose motto was "We Build, We Fight." He trained at Camp Peary, Virginia and Port Hueneme, California before shipping out to the Pacific in early 1944. His deployment took him first to the Treasury Islands, then to the Admiralty Islands, where he served until the end of the war. He rose to the rank of Chief Ship Fitter. The documents in this section trace that journey — from his first postcard home in December 1943 to the letters he wrote in the Pacific heat of June 1945.

This is not a complete account of Otto's service. It is a glimpse into the items he chose to keep.

James in the Air Corps

James Robert Litzkow — US Army Air Forces

James Robert Litzkow was born June 6, 1926 in Sauk County, Wisconsin — eighteen years old when his father enlisted, nineteen when he was inducted himself. Before the Army, he worked at Badger Ordnance Works in Baraboo, served briefly in the Wisconsin State Guard, and attended Madison West High School. He stood six feet three inches tall, with grey eyes and brown hair. His Army Serial Number was 16141259.

James trained at Bryan Army Air Field and Amarillo Army Air Field in Texas before going overseas. The documents here cover his enlistment, training, and the emergency that brought him home in August 1945 — a glimpse, not a complete account. Much of his service history remains to be discovered.

Additional history about James and other Litzkows may be added as it becomes available.

While Otto built airstrips in the Admiralty Islands and James trained on aircraft engines in Texas, Lorraine Josephine Litzkow kept the household at 1213 Hickory Street. Preservation & Provenance

About This Project

✦

The Litzkow Family Archive

This website presents a collection of letters, documents, photographs, and ephemera from the family of Otto and Lorraine Litzkow of 1213 Hickory Street, Madison, Wisconsin. The materials date primarily from 1943 to 1949 and document the family's experience of World War II — from Otto's enlistment into the Navy Seabees in October 1943 to the post-war letters of James's friend Hank Henker in 1949.

The collection was assembled, digitized, transcribed, and annotated by Christopher Litzkow — great-grandson of Otto and Lorraine Litzkow, and grandson of James Robert Litzkow and Helen Litzkow. It is presented here as a permanent family record and a contribution to the history of ordinary American families during the Second World War.

All original documents are retained by the family.

The Three Main Correspondents

Who They Were

✦
Otto John Litzkow
February 11, 1902 – January 10, 1966
U.S. Navy Seabees CBMU-587 Ship Fitter First Class (SF1/c) Pacific Theater

Otto enlisted on October 30, 1943 and trained at Camp Peary (Williamsburg, Virginia) and Port Hueneme, California before deploying to the Pacific. His unit, Construction Battalion Maintenance Unit 587, served first in the Solomon Islands (Treasury Islands) and then in the Admiralty Islands, where Otto served until the end of the war. He rose to the rank of Chief Ship Fitter.

The Coral Bits newsletter names him directly as co-designer, with Chief Ship Fitter McDermott, of a truck-mounted power thread cutter — a piece of field ingenuity that earned a full write-up in the unit paper. Otto was known to his wife Lorraine by a private salutation that appears in the June 16, 1945 letter — the most intimate document in the collection.

Otto returned to Madison after the war and died on January 10, 1966. He was 63 years old.

Lorraine Josephine Litzkow
January 22, 1906 – July 23, 1983
Home Front 1213 Hickory Street Madison, Wisconsin

Lorraine Josephine Litzkow was 37 years old when Otto enlisted, and 38 when James followed him into service. For the better part of two years, she held 1213 Hickory Street alone — managing the household finances, the allotment payments from both men, the Victory garden, and the long silences between letters.

She left no letters of her own in this collection. Her presence is felt everywhere in the letters the men wrote to her: in Otto's careful tenderness, in James's cheerful reassurances, in the Red Cross telegram that sent her son home when she fell ill in August 1945. She outlived both men by decades, dying on July 23, 1983. She was 77 years old.

James Robert Litzkow
June 6, 1926 – May 29, 1980
US Army Air Forces ASN 16141259 Bryan AAF & Amarillo AAF Texas

James was 17 years old when his father enlisted. He served briefly in the Wisconsin State Guard before his own induction into the US Army on November 30, 1944 — at 18, six feet three inches tall, with grey eyes and a character rated Excellent. He trained as an aerial mechanic, first at Bryan Army Air Field and then at Amarillo Army Air Field in Texas.

His letters home are marked by a dry humor and easy affection — signing off as "Slugger," writing to his "Hi Mom," sending along the "Me Mudder" poem on official base stationery. He never made it overseas; the war ended before his orders came through. He was brought home on emergency furlough in late August 1945, nine days before Japan surrendered, when Lorraine underwent surgery in Madison.

James married Helen Litzkow and together they had Christopher Litzkow's father. James died on May 29, 1980. He was 53 years old.

Scope & Contents

The Collection

✦

The collection comprises approximately 52 items: personal letters, V-mail, military postcards, unit newsletters, official service documents, financial records, ephemera, and photographs. Items span six years, two theaters of the war, three states (Wisconsin, Virginia/Illinois for training, Texas), and the Pacific Islands.

The core of the collection — the letters between Otto, Lorraine, and James — is presented in the Letters Home section. Documents relating specifically to Otto's Seabee service are in the Otto & the Seabees section. James's Army Air Forces service, training letters, and official records are in James in the Air Corps. Lorraine's home front materials are in The Home Front. A complete index of official documents appears in Documents & Records.

Some documents remain partially transcribed pending higher-resolution scanning. Open questions — including the identity of the young boy in the portrait photograph found with Otto's Seabees ID card — are noted where they appear in the collection.
